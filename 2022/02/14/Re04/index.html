Assessing Seismic Risk and Financial Distress in the Bay Area Hospital System
#Research 2022-02-14

Advisor: Professor Luis Ceferino (NYU), Jingzhe Wu (World Bank)
02/2022 - 08/2022

Abstract
The objective of this study is to inform decision-making with regard to Senate Bill (SB) 1953, primarily to understand the risk of each and every acute care hospital with regard to physical, financial and socio-economic vulnerabilities . Then understand the correlation between financial distress and the risk probability in case of an earthquake. Once these three vulnerabilities are quantitatively established, we will try to use these metrics to classify the hospitals and identify the extremely vulnerable hospitals that need financial assistance to complete retrofits.

A specific area in California termed as Bay Area which is considered the technology capital of the world with a population of 7.75 million is selected as the study area. The vulnerabilities of the study area are measured through three distinct research avenues

  1. Analyzing the structural vulnerability of the hospital buildings in a selected earthquake scenario.
  2. Understanding the financial vulnerability of the hospital systems.
  3. Exploring the economic and racial vulnerabilities around the bay area.

For the first stage, the study is expected to conduct an Mw 7.0 earthquake scenario simulation. The outcome of earthquake scenario models will be used to evaluate the spatial distribution of injuries and loss of functionality for assets. Combined with the result of financial analysis in stage two, the study will provide policy suggestions to enhance disaster preparedness for an effective response for hospitals in the Bay Area.

Data Collection

Data Processing

The dataset required to conduct the structural impact analysis was not unavailable at a hospital level. The CHHS seismic open data was taken as the base layer and to spatialize the data the coordinates provided in the data was used. All buildings for the same hospitals‚Äô name were given the same location leading to overlap of buildings and there were many structures like an entrance porch and connecting bridges with a separate building code. To tackle these challenges the data was manually processed to select only the main buildings and adjust the building points based on google hybrid maps background as base layer. Post this process we have spatial data with hospital names, building codes, and year of construction. To get the structure type of the building we used the building code of the hospital to search the online records submitted by hospitals for statutory records and scrape the structure type - few buildings have multiple structure types that were also scraped as a secondary structure type.

The equipment data from the AHA was at a hospital level. Therefore, we interpolate it at the building level. We used the hospital name and extracted the building footprints from the Open Street maps using the CHHS data and calculated the footprint area and assumed area as the proxy of beds and other equipment that is shared within hospitals. A raster layer of soil was obtained from the database of OpenSHA. We georeferenced the raster and by using natural neighbor interpolation method to finally create the dataset required for structural damage analysis.

Methodology for Seismic Analysis

The methodology is divided into three stages: performing seismic hazard analysis in the San Francisco Bay Area, modeling hospital damage, and assessing the economic losses of hospital facilities due to seismic hazards.

Methodology for Financial Analysis

In this part, we choose to use the Altman Z-score model to analyze the hospital‚Äôs financial distress according to the annual financial report.

The Altman Z-score is a widely used and successful method for categorizing the financial health of an organization. The Altman Z score model was first used in 1968. At first, this is a discriminant analysis regression model of financial ratio variables used to predict corporate bankruptcies.

Here we use this conventional model that is used to analyze the California hospital‚Äôs financial distress. We choose this regression model for its simplicity and explainability. The factors are defined as follows:

ùë•1 represents a liquidity ratio, which is important for a hospital‚Äôs usual operating expenses. This factor is calculated by current assets minus current liabilities and then divided by total assets.

ùë•2 represents a measure of financial leverage. We use this factor to focus on the liability proportion of total assets. This factor is calculated by total assets minus total liabilities and then divided by total assets.

ùë•3 is a measure of profitability and asset productivity. This factor is calculated by total revenue minus total expenses before taxes and then divided by total assets.

ùë•4 is the book value of equity as a proportion of total liabilities.

When we calculate all the factors for each hospital, we put them in our regression equation and get the final Z-score for each hospital. And according to the Altman Z-score model, we defined that when Z-score is less than 1.8, hospitals in this range are in serious financial distress, when Z-score is more than 3.0, hospitals in this range are in non-financial distress, and when Z-score between 1.8 and 3.0, hospitals in this range have potential financial distress. Langabeer et al. (2018) use 3.0 and 1.8 as Z thresholds, as applied to acute care hospitals. We use the same cutoffs in this analysis.

Results for Earthquake Analysis

In this section, we describe the results from our early data exploration and three parts of scenario modeling. The building dataset initially contains 1418 building blocks in the entire California state. After constraining their geolocation to stay within the Bay Area and removing deprecated data points, there are 87 hospital buildings left in our dataset. The majority of buildings are built after 1940, as shown in left figure. Building stocks vary from one-story clinics to tall comprehensive hospital facilities, as shown in right figure.

Using the mean PGA obtained from the SimCenter R2D HAZ tool and grid information, every building is assigned a ground-shaking measure based on its nearest grid, as shown in the following figure. The assigned PGA will be used as the intensity measurements to determine the damaged state of buildings under our given scenario.

On average, most buildings are not damaged. However, when we plot the probability distribution of damage states for every building in the following figure, some buildings have significantly higher seismic risk than the majority. Out of all 57 hospitals we considered, 29 hospitals have a higher possibility of severe damage. These hospitals are Alameda Hospital, Fairmont Hospital, Highland Hospital, etc. The result suggests that we could consider prioritizing the needs of these hospitals while allocating funds and resources to seismic upgrades.

Results for Financial Analysis

From the below figure , we make a rank based on the Z-score from the lowest to the highest. 47% of hospitals in the Bay area are in non-financial distress, which is a good signal for this area and this is also positive for the construction legislation that hospitals have to comply with 17% of hospitals are in potential financial distress, this number is really small according to the whole percentage. 36% of hospitals are in serious financial distress.

As shown in the following table, financially-sustainable hospitals marked in the green box make up nearly 80% of the multi-hospital system. Under this situation, these hospitals have influential funds. Individual hospitals generate enough benefits to retrofit their buildings to meet the SB 1953 and will finally reach the highest ratings of NPC-5 and SPC-5.

Hospitals located in the yellow box with potential financial distress are not in the multi-hospital system. Compared to previous figure, this part occupied a small portion base on the whole dataset. Therefore, these out-of-multi-hospital system hospitals are using their own revenues to reconstruct their buildings to meet the policy. Their financial condition can be considered as the normal financial conditions in our report.

Hospitals located in the red box in the table are in serious financial distress. Some hospitals are individual operation hospitals. However, other hospitals even in the large multi-hospital system are in serious financial distress, especially hospitals in the Alameda multi-hospital system. This phenomenon indicates that there is an unbalanced distribution of finance in the multi-hospital system since the Alameda Health System has both the best and worst financial condition hospitals.
