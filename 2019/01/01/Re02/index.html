Difference Study on Risks of Agricultural Commodities under Sino-US Trade Conflicts (Published)
#Research#Published Article 2019-01-01

Research Focus:
This study examined international agricultural commodity futures trading prices, specifically focusing on the risk variations in international agricultural commodity prices amid the differences in Sino-American trade friction.

The research collected daily price data for four major agricultural commodities - corn, soybeans, wheat, and pork - from March 2018 to July 2019. High-frequency data processing was applied to obtain return rates through log differencing, which were then analyzed using measures such as median, mean, skewness, and kurtosis.

Stationarity tests and ARCH effect tests confirmed that the time series data were suitable for GARCH modeling. Model orders were determined based on AIC criteria, and various GARCH models within the parameter class were explored. These models included GARCH(1,1), IGARCH(1,1), and APARCH(1,1).

Key Findings:
From the modeling results, several key conclusions were drawn:

  1. The GARCH(1,1) model indicated that external shocks had a relatively persistent impact, with long-term trade frictions having a severe impact on the national economy.
  2. The VaR values for corn, wheat, and soybeans decreased with longer holding periods, implying that trade frictions were slightly overestimated in their effect on futures markets. In contrast, APARCH model-based VaR risk values provided a more realistic depiction of volatility in the futures market.
  3. For both normal and t-distribution assumptions, the sum of ARCH and GARCH coefficients was close to 1, indicating that external shocks had a relatively persistent impact on the futures prices.
  4. In the GARCH(1,1) model results, the pork futures market was more significantly affected by negative news, and the VaR values calculated under the IGARCH model exceeded those under the APARCH model.

Risk Prediction and Conclusion:
Conducted the predictions in the study to predict the risk values for futures prices for periods of 1, 5, 30, 90, and 180. In terms of short-term price volatility, soybean futures exhibited the least risk, which correlated with its attributes, such as low storage and transportation costs, making it a versatile trading commodity. In contrast, pork futures showed greater risk due to their pronounced cyclical nature, higher storage and transportation costs, and less flexible trading markets.

Predicted VaR risk values:
In the medium to long term (two months and beyond), soybean futures remained the least risky, while pork futures were the riskiest among the commodities.

At a 95% confidence level, and considering observation intervals from 1 period to 180 periods into the future, the study evaluated the occurrence frequency of special events for each agricultural product.

Comparing the occurrence frequency of special events with the previously discussed VaR values, it was observed that VaR for corn futures decreased with longer time horizons. This indicated that the VaR was met at a 95% confidence level, confirming successful backtesting. Similar success was observed for wheat and soybean futures, suggesting that their return rate models align well with the GARCH models, yielding positive results. However, pork futures demonstrated less ideal outcomes, indicating that factors affecting pork commodity futures prices may extend beyond market forces.
